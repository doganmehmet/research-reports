Late-Cycle Exuberance Meets Policy Fog: Growth Stocks at a Crossroads

Global equity markets have delivered a mixed first quarter of 2026, with the MSCI World Index pulling back roughly 4% from its December 2025 highs to around 4,370 as of mid-March, after a strong 2025 that saw the index climb over 20% for the year. Gold has been the standout performer, surging past $5,000/oz in early 2026 — a move reminiscent of the late-1970s flight to hard assets, though today’s drivers are more about sovereign debt concerns and geopolitical hedging than runaway inflation. Global yields have been grinding lower as central banks outside the U.S. resume easing, with the 10-year Treasury settling near 4.15% and the 2-year around 3.59%, producing a positively sloped curve for the first time in years. Brent crude remains subdued in the low-to-mid $60s, reflecting tepid global industrial demand that contrasts with the resilience of U.S. services and tech sectors. The pattern — strong equity gains concentrated in a narrow set of U.S. mega-cap growth names, paired with weakening commodities and rotating global capital — echoes dynamics we saw in 1998-99 and again in 2019, both periods that preceded meaningful regime shifts.

The U.S. growth stock landscape entering mid-March 2026 sits in a fascinating and somewhat uncomfortable position. The S&P 500 closed February near 5,882 and has since retreated to approximately 5,612 in early March, while the Nasdaq 100 has pulled back more sharply from its January peak of ~25,553 to around 24,655. Year-to-date, the S&P 500 is down roughly 5% from its 2025 close of 5,881, and the Nasdaq 100 has given back about 3-4%. These are not catastrophic drawdowns by any measure, but after two consecutive years of 20%+ returns for the S&P 500 (2024 and 2025), even a modest correction feels significant to portfolios that have become heavily tilted toward U.S. growth.

The valuation picture is where things start to get uncomfortable. The S&P 500 trailing P/E ratio stands at approximately 28.8x as of recent data, which places us in rarified territory. Since 1871, trailing P/E ratios above 28x have been sustained for extended periods only twice: during the dot-com bubble (1998-2001, peaking above 44x) and during the post-COVID stimulus era (2020-2021, peaking around 39x). The current reading of ~29x isn’t at those extremes, but it’s firmly in the top decile of historical observations. For context, the long-run median trailing P/E is roughly 15-16x, and even the elevated 1990s average was around 22-25x. We’re about 75-90% above the long-run median.

What makes the current valuation stretch more nuanced than a simple “it’s expensive, sell” call is the earnings environment. Unlike the dot-com era where P/E expansion was driven largely by speculative enthusiasm with deteriorating earnings (the P/E spiked to 44x as earnings collapsed in 2001-02), today’s high P/E is supported by genuinely strong corporate earnings, particularly among mega-cap tech. GDP growth, while moderating — Q4 2025 came in at just 0.7% annualized after a strong mid-year — remains positive, and the labor market at 4.4% unemployment in early 2026 is healthy by any historical standard. The comparison I keep returning to is late 1999 to early 2000: unemployment was 4.0%, GDP was growing at 2-4%, inflation was benign around 2-3%, and trailing P/E ratios were 28-30x before the final blowoff took them to 44x. We have the same ingredients minus the parabolic retail frenzy (so far).

The yield curve tells an interesting story that deserves careful attention. After being inverted from mid-2022 through much of 2024 — one of the longest sustained inversions since the early 1980s — the 2Y/10Y spread has finally turned positive, with the 10-year at ~4.15% and the 2-year at ~3.59%, producing a spread of roughly +56 basis points. Historically, the normalization of an inverted curve has been a mixed signal. The inversion itself is the warning; the steepening that follows often coincides with the onset of economic weakness, as the Fed cuts the short end in response to deteriorating conditions. This is exactly what happened in 2001 (curve normalized as the recession began), in 2007-08 (same pattern), and in 2020 (albeit compressed into weeks). The Fed has cut three times since September 2024, bringing the upper bound to 4.50% by December 2024, and then delivered three more cuts in late 2025 to reach 3.75%. The September-December 2025 cuts — 75bps total — suggest the Fed saw enough softening to act preemptively. For growth investors, the key question is whether these cuts are “insurance” cuts (like 1995 or 1998) that extend the cycle, or “too late” cuts (like 2001 or 2007) that merely cushion the landing.

The inflation backdrop supports the more benign interpretation — for now. CPI has settled around 2.4% year-over-year as of early 2026, with core CPI at 2.5%. This is a dramatic improvement from the 9.0% peak in mid-2022 and represents the smoothest disinflation in a post-surge cycle since the 1950s. Critically, current inflation levels are almost identical to the 2017-2019 pre-pandemic range (core CPI 2.0-2.4%), which was a Goldilocks environment for growth stocks. The parallel to 1995-96 is also compelling: after the Fed hiked aggressively from 1994-95, inflation settled into the 2.5-3.0% range, the economy achieved a soft landing, and equities — particularly growth and technology — embarked on one of the greatest bull runs in history. The difference today is that we’re starting from far higher valuations than the 15-16x P/E of 1995.

The Fed’s balance sheet adds another layer of complexity that didn’t exist in earlier cycles. Total assets stand at approximately $6.6 trillion as of early 2026, down from the peak of ~$8.95 trillion in mid-2022 but still roughly 9x the pre-2008 level and nearly 3x the pre-COVID level. Quantitative tightening (QT) has been running since 2022, shrinking the balance sheet by over $2.3 trillion — a remarkable achievement that has occurred without a financial accident. But the pace of QT has slowed considerably in recent months, and the Fed appears to be approaching what it considers the “ample reserves” floor. The closest historical analogue is the 2017-2019 QT cycle, which ended abruptly in September 2019 with repo market stress and forced the Fed to pivot to balance sheet expansion. Growth stocks sold off 20% in Q4 2018 during peak QT anxiety before rallying sharply when the Fed reversed course. The current balance sheet runway is a tail risk — not the central case, but one that could amplify any correction if reserve scarcity triggers money market dysfunction.

Credit markets are telling us that the corporate sector remains healthy, but with some early yellow flags. The BofA High Yield Option-Adjusted Spread stands at approximately 3.28% as of the latest reading — tight by historical standards and consistent with a benign credit environment. For reference, spreads were 2.5-3.0% in 2007 before exploding to 20% during the GFC, and 3.1-3.5% in 2019 before the COVID shock took them to 8.8%. Current spreads are slightly wider than the cycle tights of ~2.7% reached in late 2024, which could represent either a healthy repricing or the early stages of a more meaningful widening. In prior cycles, the transition from “tight” to “blowing out” happened quickly — it took just 6 months in 2007-08 for spreads to go from 3% to 9%. Growth stocks are not immune to credit stress; the 2018 Q4 selloff was triggered in part by credit spread widening, and even high-quality growth names were hit hard.

The unemployment picture provides perhaps the most reassuring data point for bulls. At 4.4% as of the most recent reading (January 2026), the labor market has softened from the 3.4% tights of early 2023 but remains well within the range that supports consumer spending and corporate revenue growth. The trajectory — gradually rising from 3.4% to 4.4% over three years — mirrors the 1996-2000 period, when unemployment drifted from 5.5% to 3.8% (in that case declining), and the 2017-2019 period, when it fell from 4.4% to 3.5%. In both cases, the equity market performed well as long as unemployment wasn’t accelerating upward. The risk signal from unemployment isn’t the level but the rate of change: when unemployment rises by more than 50bps in a 6-month period, recessions have followed in nearly every case since 1948 (the “Sahm Rule”). The current pace of increase is at the boundary of that threshold, which is why I’m watching the monthly jobs reports more closely than the Fed minutes.

Gold’s extraordinary run to $5,000+ deserves attention from growth investors, even if we don’t hold any. Gold has essentially tripled from its 2022 lows near $1,600, and the current price represents a move of over 80% in the last two years alone. This is the most aggressive gold rally since 2009-2011, and the magnitude begins to approach the 1976-1980 episode (a 7x move). Historically, gold rallies of this scale have coincided with late-cycle dynamics: rising fiscal deficits, central bank credibility concerns, or geopolitical regime changes. The 1970s gold surge preceded the Volcker recession; the 2009-2011 surge accompanied the post-GFC recovery but signaled the inflationary potential of QE. Today’s gold move likely reflects a combination of de-dollarization fears, fiscal deficit anxiety (U.S. deficit/GDP remains elevated), and portfolio rebalancing by sovereign wealth funds. For growth stocks, the signal is ambiguous: gold can surge while equities rally (2019-2020), but extended gold outperformance often signals that real returns on financial assets will disappoint.

Household debt service payments as a percentage of disposable income have edged up to 11.26% — the highest since the pre-pandemic level of ~11.7% and rising. While still well below the 2007 peak of 15.85% and even the 2005-2006 levels around 15%, the trend is upward and bears watching. The post-COVID deleveraging that took this ratio to 9.05% (a historic low) provided a massive tailwind for consumer spending and, by extension, growth stock revenues. That tailwind is fading. The current trajectory is similar to 2016-2019, when debt service ratios gradually climbed from 11.5% to 11.7% without triggering a consumer recession, but the combination of higher rates and rising credit card delinquencies makes this a more fragile equilibrium.

The VIX at 23.5 as of mid-March 2026 reflects elevated but not panicked uncertainty. For context, the VIX averaged 12-14 through most of 2017 and 2019, was in the 16-20 range during the “normal” pullbacks of 2015-2016, and spiked above 30 during genuine crises (2008, 2011, 2018 Q4, 2020). The current level is consistent with a market that is digesting uncertainty around tariff policy, AI monetization timelines, and the macro cycle — but hasn’t capitulated. Notably, the VIX pattern resembles mid-2015: moderately elevated volatility during a correction that ultimately proved to be a buying opportunity, though it took several months of chop before the all-clear.

Putting the pieces together and drawing on the historical pattern library, I see the most relevant comparison for today’s U.S. growth market as a blend of 1998-99 and 2018-19, with some less comfortable echoes of 1999-2000:

The 1998-99 parallel centers on the Fed cutting rates into an already strong economy with tight credit spreads, elevated valuations, and narrow market leadership. In 1998, the Fed cut three times (75bps total) to cushion the LTCM/Asian crisis shock; the economy re-accelerated, and the Nasdaq surged 85% over the next 18 months before collapsing. Today’s Fed has cut 175bps from peak, the economy hasn’t truly faltered, and growth stock valuations are stretched. The risk is that rate cuts fuel a final melt-up followed by a severe correction.

The 2018-19 parallel captures the late-cycle tightening overshoot, market correction, policy pivot, and subsequent rally. The S&P 500 fell 20% in Q4 2018, the Fed reversed from hiking to cutting, and equities rallied 30% in 2019. The current correction (~5% from highs) is milder, but the Fed’s pivot to cutting is analogous, and the setup for a rally is similar if economic data stabilizes.

The uncomfortable 1999-2000 echo is the valuation story. The current trailing P/E of ~29x is almost exactly where the S&P 500 traded in early 1999 (29-30x) before the final blowoff to 44x. The market can stay expensive longer than bears expect — 1999 taught us that viscerally — but the eventual reversion was brutal (-49% for the S&P 500, -83% for the Nasdaq 100).

Key Risks for Growth Investors:

  • Valuation compression without an earnings collapse. Even if corporate earnings grow 8-10% annually, a P/E reversion from 29x to 22x (still above the long-run average) would produce a flat-to-negative total return over 2-3 years. This is what happened in 2000-2002 and again in 2022. With the 10-year yield at 4.15%, the equity risk premium is thin (~1.3%), which historically has been a precondition for P/E compression. The current configuration — S&P 500 earnings yield of ~3.5% vs. a 10-year yield of 4.15% — is essentially an inverted equity risk premium, last seen persistently in 1999-2000.

  • Economic deceleration beyond the soft landing. Q4 2025 GDP growth of just 0.7% annualized, after a strong mid-year, suggests momentum is fading. If unemployment breaches 4.6-4.7% and the Sahm Rule triggers, the recession probability increases sharply. Growth stocks — particularly the unprofitable cohort — historically draw down 40-60% in recessions (2001, 2008, 2020). The pattern of the yield curve normalizing after a prolonged inversion, combined with declining GDP growth and gradually rising unemployment, matches the 2006-2008 pre-recession sequence with a concerning degree of fidelity.

Key Opportunities for Growth Investors:

  • The mid-cycle extension playbook. If the Fed’s cuts are successful in re-accelerating growth (similar to 1995 and 1998), the next 12-18 months could see significant upside, particularly for high-quality growth companies with pricing power and secular demand drivers like AI infrastructure, cloud computing, and digital advertising. The 1995 soft landing produced a 230% cumulative return in the S&P 500 over the subsequent five years, with the Nasdaq 100 returning over 1,000%. Today’s AI investment cycle has clear parallels to the internet infrastructure buildout of 1996-1999, and the monetization phase may be ahead of us, not behind us.

  • Volatility as an entry point. The current VIX of ~23.5 and the 5-8% pullback in the Nasdaq 100 from highs represent a more attractive entry point than the sub-14 VIX and all-time-high prices of late 2024. Historically, buying the S&P 500 when the VIX is between 20-25 (after being below 15) has produced above-average 12-month forward returns approximately 65-70% of the time. The current correction, if it doesn’t escalate into a credit event or recession, is the type of reset that long-term growth investors should welcome.

The Brent crude picture reinforces the deflationary/disinflationary backdrop that has historically been supportive of growth stock multiples. At $63-65/barrel, oil is well below the $80-100 range that prevailed through much of 2022-2024, removing a cost headwind for consumers and corporations alike. Low oil prices in 1997-99 were a major enabler of the tech-led equity rally, and while today’s energy market dynamics are different (U.S. shale makes supply more elastic), the net effect on corporate margins and consumer spending is similarly supportive.

The household debt trajectory warrants monitoring but isn’t yet flashing red. The current 11.26% debt service ratio remains below the 11.5-11.7% range that prevailed through 2017-2019 without triggering consumer distress. However, the rate of increase matters: this ratio has climbed ~2.2 percentage points from the COVID low of 9.05%, primarily driven by higher interest rates on variable-rate debt (credit cards, auto loans). If the Fed’s rate cuts flow through to consumer borrowing costs, this pressure should moderate. The risk is that it doesn’t — if rates stay “higher for longer” while income growth slows, the household sector could become a drag on growth stock revenues, particularly in consumer discretionary and fintech.

In synthesizing all of this, the honest assessment is that U.S. growth stocks are in a late-cycle environment with above-average return potential but significantly above-average risk. The data doesn’t suggest an imminent crash — credit spreads are tight, unemployment is low, inflation is controlled, and the Fed has room to cut further. But the valuations, the slowing GDP trajectory, the rising unemployment trend, and the gold market’s alarm bells all counsel against maximum aggression.

Suggested Portfolio Exposure to U.S. Growth Stocks: 50-65%

This is a “stay invested but not all-in” stance. The bull case for growth stocks remains intact — the AI cycle is real, corporate balance sheets are healthy, and the Fed put is active — but the margin of safety at 29x trailing earnings is thin. I’d keep a meaningful allocation to growth (particularly profitable, free-cash-flow-generating companies), maintain some cash or short-duration bonds as dry powder for a deeper correction, and avoid the temptation to lever into the highest-beta names. The 1995-96 analogy is seductive, but so is the 1999-2000 one. We won’t know which playbook is running until after the fact, and the responsible approach is to position for both outcomes. If the S&P 500 corrects another 10-15% from here with credit spreads staying contained, I’d look to increase exposure toward 75%. If valuations re-rate higher from here without earnings growth to match, I’d trim toward 40%.
