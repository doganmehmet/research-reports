Navigating the Peak: A Growth Investor’s Guide to Late-Cycle Terrain

Global financial markets continued their powerful rally through the third quarter of 2025, fueled by resilient corporate earnings and rising expectations for a dovish pivot from central banks. While U.S. markets posted strong returns, with the S&P 500 gaining roughly 8% for the quarter, international markets in China and Japan saw even more robust performance. This broad equity strength came as bond yields fell and the Federal Reserve initiated its first rate cut of the year, steepening the yield curve. This environment of rallying equities, a newly accommodative Fed, and a steepening yield curve presents a complex picture reminiscent of previous late-cycle transitions.

The current landscape for U.S. growth stocks evokes strong parallels to the dot-com era of the late 1990s. Valuations, a critical metric for growth investors, have become notably stretched. The S&P 500’s P/E ratio now stands at a lofty 30.38, a level that mirrors the peak valuations seen just before the market downturn in 2000-2001. This is particularly relevant for the Nasdaq 100, which has surged to 24,679, capping an extraordinary multi-year run that echoes the tech-fueled exuberance of the late 90s. This valuation expansion has been underpinned by a monetary policy cycle that is also rhyming with history. After a rapid series of rate hikes that took the Federal Funds Target Rate to a peak of 5.5% in 2023, the Fed has recently pivoted, delivering rate cuts in September 2025. This shift from aggressive tightening to easing is a classic late-cycle signal that often precedes periods of economic and market volatility.

Further adding to the sense of déjà vu is the behavior of the U.S. Treasury yield curve. After a prolonged period of inversion throughout 2023 and early 2024—a historically reliable recession indicator—the curve has now decisively steepened. The spread between the 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields has widened to approximately +0.55%, a sharp reversal from the negative readings. While an inverted curve warns of a coming recession, history shows that the subsequent steepening often occurs as the downturn begins and the Fed starts cutting rates. This pattern was clearly visible in 2000 and 2007, making the current steepening a critical signal for growth investors to monitor.

However, not all historical comparisons point to an imminent collapse. When measured against the prelude to the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the health of the U.S. consumer provides a notable source of stability. The Household Debt Service Ratio, which measures debt payments as a percentage of disposable income, currently stands at a manageable 11.25%. This is significantly below the 15.8% peak reached in 2007, suggesting that consumers are not nearly as over-leveraged as they were heading into the last major crisis. This relative strength in household finances could provide a crucial buffer for corporate earnings, even if economic growth slows.

This complex environment of high valuations and late-cycle signals, juxtaposed with a newly dovish Fed and a resilient consumer, presents both clear risks and distinct opportunities for U.S. growth investors.

  • Risk: Valuation Contraction. The S&P 500’s P/E ratio of over 30 is in rarefied air, territory last seen during the dot-com bubble. Growth stocks, which trade at even higher multiples, are acutely vulnerable to a reversion to the mean. Should earnings growth disappoint or inflation prove sticky, forcing the Fed to pause its easing cycle, these stocks could face a significant de-rating.

  • Risk: The Recessionary Un-inversion. The steepening of the 2Y/10Y yield curve, from deep inversion to its current positive slope, has historically been a final warning shot before a recession. If this pattern holds true, the subsequent economic slowdown would pressure corporate earnings and revenue growth, undermining the fundamental thesis for many growth stocks.

  • Opportunity: The Soft Landing. The Federal Reserve has begun cutting rates with unemployment still at a relatively low 4.3% and GDP growth positive. If policymakers successfully navigate a “soft landing”—cooling inflation without triggering a recession—this new easing cycle could extend the bull market. Lower rates reduce the discount rate on future earnings, providing a powerful tailwind for growth stock valuations.

  • Opportunity: Resilient Consumer Spending. Unlike the pre-GFC period, household balance sheets are not flashing red. A healthy consumer can continue to spend, supporting revenue growth for companies in sectors like technology, e-commerce, and discretionary goods. This fundamental strength could allow top-tier growth companies to continue delivering strong results even in a more challenging macroeconomic environment.

Portfolio Recommendation

Considering the conflicting signals, a balanced approach to U.S. growth stock exposure is warranted. The exceptionally high valuations and ominous historical precedents set by the yield curve call for a degree of caution. However, the Federal Reserve’s dovish pivot and the relative health of the U.S. consumer provide a compelling reason to remain invested in secular growth trends. The momentum is undeniably strong, and exiting the market completely could mean missing out on significant further gains if a soft landing is achieved.

Therefore, a 50-75% allocation to U.S. growth stocks appears appropriate. This allows for meaningful participation in the upside potential driven by the new easing cycle while acknowledging that we are likely in the later innings of this bull market, where risks are elevated and downside protection should be a key consideration.
