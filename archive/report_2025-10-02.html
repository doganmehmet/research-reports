OPEANAI GBT-5

Steepening, Soft-Landing—or Second Late‑Cycle? What It Means for US Growth (Oct 2, 2025)

Global markets over the last three months have been risk‑on. MSCI World rose about 5.7% in Q3 (Jul–Sep) and is up roughly 16.7% year‑to‑date through today; the S&P 500 gained ~5.5% in Q3 and ~14.2% YTD, while the Nasdaq‑100 is up ~18.5% YTD. US yields have re‑steepened: the 10Y is near 4.12% and the 2s10s spread is about +57 bps (positive again after last year’s inversion). The Fed has cut a cumulative 125 bps since September 2024 (upper bound now 4.25%), while QT continues and global developed‑market yields have drifted off their 2023–early 2025 highs. The mix—rising equities, a modestly steeper curve, and easier policy—looks reminiscent of several past late‑cycle “calm before the storm” phases as well as 1995‑style soft‑landing windows.

US growth investor’s lens: today vs past cycles—what rhymes, what matters Valuation sits at the heart of the setup. The S&P 500 P/E is about 31.1 (latest monthly reading), placing it in the same zip code as 1999–2000 and above 2018–2019. That doesn’t preordain trouble, but it tells you what’s driving returns: multiple expansion more than earnings.

Macro signals are mixed, not weak. Headline CPI is back in the high‑2s (Aug: 2.9% YoY; core ~3.1%), unemployment has cooled to a still‑low but rising 4.3% (Aug), and real growth re‑accelerated (2025 Q2 GDP YoY: 3.8% after a soft Q1). That’s closer to “mid‑cycle deceleration” than contraction, with productivity and margins doing more work than raw top‑line growth.

A notable change: the curve has flipped back to positive. The 2Y sits near 3.55% and the 10Y near 4.12%, putting the 2s10s spread at roughly +0.57%. That’s eerily similar to early 2001 (+0.54% in March 2001) and to 1994–95’s post‑inversion steepening, when the Fed eased and duration assets breathed. How that steepening occurs matters: steepening via front‑end cuts (like now) can be bullish for long‑duration growth if long rates stay anchored; if long rates back up, multiples compress.

Policy is easier, liquidity less so. The Fed has delivered four cuts since September 2024 (from 5.5% to 4.25%), while continuing to shrink its balance sheet—down to roughly $6.6T from about $7.7T at end‑2023. That blend (rate cuts + QT) rhymes more with 1998–1999 than 2013 or 2018: it loosens the front end without unleashing the same flood of systemic liquidity we saw in 2020–2021.

Positioning and sentiment feel complacent, not euphoric. VIX has bled back into the mid‑teens after an April spike above 30. That’s classic for late‑cycle or soft‑landing tapes: realized volatility fades, leadership narrows to quality compounders and mega‑cap platforms, and pullbacks are bought quickly—until a macro or earnings surprise breaks the pattern.

The equity tape confirms the leadership story. Mega‑cap growth has pulled the major indices higher into Q3; breadth has improved vs late‑2023 but remains uneven. Year‑to‑date, the S&P 500 and Nasdaq‑100 advances have outpaced global DM ex‑US, consistent with US growth duration benefiting first from policy re‑pricing.

How today resembles past boom‑bust nodes—and why it matters for growth stocks - Valuation altitude: Today’s ~31.1 P/E is near the 1999–2000 band (roughly 28–34). In both 1999 and 2020–2021, growth leadership persisted while the multiple was expanding, but forward returns became acutely sensitive to small changes in discount rates and earnings revisions. That’s our starting point now.

  • Curve regime change: The current +0.57% 2s10s spread (Oct 2025) resembles the steepening that followed the 2000 inversion (e.g., +0.54% in March 2001) and the 1994–95 soft landing (2s10s around +0.45% by mid‑1995). Relevance: when steepening is led by front‑end cuts and 10Y stability, growth’s duration often gets a tailwind; when steepening is driven by a 10Y back‑up, long‑duration equities can see 1–3 turns of P/E compression quickly.

  • Growth without overheating: CPI near 2.9% YoY and unemployment at 4.3% echo 1995 more than 2007; inflation is moderating but not collapsing, and labor markets are loosening at the margin. For growth investors, that combination historically supports premium multiples—until either inflation re‑accelerates (2018) or labor weakens enough to hit top‑line (2001).

  • Liquidity and QT: Fed assets are down ~-$1.1T from late‑2023; in 2018 QT coincided with multiple compression and a Q4 air‑pocket despite solid earnings. Today’s offset is rate cuts (and a steeper curve), which is closer to the 1998–99 mix that extended the cycle but also raised fragility.

  • Safety valve in households: Debt‑service ratios (~11.25% of income in 2025 Q2) remain far below 2007 peaks (~15.8%). That reduces the tail risk of sudden consumer retrenchment, bolstering secular growers with consumer exposure.

Where the analogues diverge Unlike 1999, the real policy rate is not deeply negative and the 10Y is ~4.1%, not ~6.5%—a check on runaway multiple expansion. Unlike 2018, inflation is not accelerating and the Fed is cutting, not hiking. And unlike 2021, liquidity is not gushing in at the same scale, which should temper the “everything rerates up” impulse and favor profitable growth over speculative stories.

What this means for positioning right now - Expect earnings and rates to co‑drive returns. With the S&P 500 at ~14% YTD and the P/E already at ~31, further index gains will likely require 2026 EPS revisions higher or another 25–50 bps drop in the 10Y; absent that, returns skew more idiosyncratic.

  • Favor quality duration over speculative duration. A steeper curve from front‑end cuts plus a 10Y anchored near 4% historically tilts the field toward profitable platforms (free cash flow, operating leverage) vs pre‑profit narratives.

  • Use volatility, don’t chase it. Mid‑teens VIX implies cheap optionality for hedges and for adding on pullbacks; April’s spike reminded us that regime shifts can still surface.

Key risks and opportunities for US growth investors - Risks - Valuation compression from a long‑rate back‑up: if the 10Y revisits ~4.6% (its January 2025 area) from ~4.12% today, a 40–50 bps move could shave 2–3 P/E turns from a ~31 multiple—roughly a 6–10% price hit absent offsetting EPS upgrades. This echoes 2018’s pattern and late 1999 sensitivity. - “Steepening into slowdown”: the 2s10s at +0.57% looks like early‑2001 (+0.54% in March 2001); if unemployment were to rise another ~0.7–1.0 pp from 4.3% (Aug) over the next few quarters, growth EPS would be at risk even as the front end eases—classic late‑cycle trap.

  • Opportunities
    • Duration boost from stable long rates: with CPI in the high‑2s and the Fed cutting (upper bound 4.25%), a drift of the 10Y toward ~3.75–4.00% could add 1–2 turns to high‑quality growth P/Es, supporting mid‑teens total returns even on modest EPS growth.
    • Resilient demand base: GDP YoY re‑acceleration to 3.8% in Q2 2025, plus household debt service near ~11.25%, creates a cushion for secular growers to compound through macro chop, reminiscent of 1995–97 where quality tech outperformed without mania‑level liquidity.

Bottom line and portfolio stance Given the late‑cycle but not‑yet‑overheated mix—positive curve, moderating inflation, resilient growth, stretched valuations—I’m constructive but not max risk. I’d hold a growth allocation of 50–75% of equity exposure, skewed to profitable compounders and cash‑rich platforms, with optionality to add on any rate‑driven selloff. That range acknowledges real upside if long rates stay anchored while allowing room to buy the dip if a 1998‑style volatility shock or a 2018‑style QT wobble hits.

GEMINI 2.5-Pro

The End of an Era: Navigating the Post-Bubble Landscape

Global financial markets have navigated a complex environment over the past three months, characterized by moderating but still elevated inflation, divergent central bank policies, and ongoing geopolitical tensions. Global equities saw modest gains, though performance was uneven across regions, with U.S. markets, particularly the technology sector, showing continued leadership. Government bond yields have remained high as central banks signal a cautious approach to monetary easing in the face of persistent inflation. This backdrop of slowing but still positive growth, coupled with hawkish central bank commentary, has created an environment reminiscent of the periods preceding major market shifts in the United States.

As a U.S. growth-stock investor, the current landscape presents a fascinating and challenging picture. While the initial “boom” phase of the cycle appears to be maturing, a “bust” is not necessarily imminent. However, a careful analysis of historical data reveals compelling parallels to past market cycles that warrant a cautious and selective approach to growth investing.

The most striking resemblance to a past “boom and bust” period is the dot-com bubble of the late 1990s and early 2000s. The current S&P 500 P/E ratio, standing at 31.12 as of October 2025, while not at the dizzying heights of the dot-com peak, is significantly above its historical average. This elevated valuation is particularly pronounced in the Nasdaq 100, which has been driven by a handful of mega-cap technology stocks, mirroring the narrow leadership seen in the late 90s.

Furthermore, the recent inversion of the 2-year and 10-year Treasury yields, with the 2-year yield at 3.55% and the 10-year at 4.12% as of October 2, 2025, echoes the yield curve dynamics seen in the lead-up to the 2000 recession. While the spread is not as deeply negative as it was then, the inversion itself has historically been a reliable recession indicator.

Another parallel can be drawn to the pre-Global Financial Crisis period of 2006-2007. While household debt service payments as a percentage of disposable personal income are currently at a manageable 11.25%, any significant increase in unemployment from the current 4.3% could strain household budgets and lead to a pullback in consumer spending, a key driver of economic growth.

The Federal Reserve’s current monetary policy also presents a point of comparison. After a period of aggressive rate hikes to combat inflation, which peaked at 9.0% in June 2022, the Fed has recently begun to ease, with the target rate now at 4.25%. This pivot from tightening to easing is a common feature of late-cycle dynamics, as the central bank attempts to navigate a “soft landing.” However, the timing and pace of these cuts will be critical; moving too slowly could stifle growth, while cutting too quickly could reignite inflationary pressures.

These historical resemblances are relevant for U.S. growth stocks for several reasons. High valuations make these stocks vulnerable to a significant correction if earnings growth disappoints or if a recession materializes. A flight to safety, typical during economic downturns, would likely see investors rotate out of high-beta growth names and into more defensive sectors. The current low reading of the Cboe Volatility Index (VIX) at 16.46 suggests a degree of complacency in the market, which could exacerbate a sell-off if a negative catalyst emerges.

Despite these risks, there are also opportunities for discerning growth investors.

  • Opportunity: Innovation and Disruption: Periods of economic transition often accelerate innovation. Companies at the forefront of secular growth trends such as artificial intelligence, biotechnology, and renewable energy may continue to deliver strong earnings growth even in a slower macroeconomic environment.
  • Opportunity: Quality Growth: In a more uncertain market, investors are likely to favor companies with strong balance sheets, consistent profitability, and durable competitive advantages. Identifying these “quality growth” stocks could provide a degree of resilience in a downturn.
  • Risk: Valuation Compression: The primary risk for growth stocks is a contraction in their valuation multiples. If risk appetite wanes, investors may be unwilling to pay the high premiums currently commanded by many growth stocks, leading to significant price declines even if the underlying business fundamentals remain solid.
  • Risk: Economic Sensitivity: Many growth sectors, such as technology and consumer discretionary, are sensitive to the economic cycle. A recession would likely lead to a slowdown in consumer and business spending, impacting the earnings of these companies.

Given the current market environment, a more selective and cautious approach to U.S. growth stocks is warranted. The parallels to past “boom and bust” cycles, particularly the high valuations and inverted yield curve, suggest that the risks of a significant market downturn have increased. However, the underlying strength of the U.S. economy and the potential for continued innovation present opportunities for long-term investors.

Suggested Portfolio Exposure for US Growth Stocks: 25-50%

This reduced exposure reflects the elevated risks in the current market. A 25-50% allocation allows for participation in the potential upside of innovative growth companies while providing a buffer in the event of a market correction. The focus within this allocation should be on high-quality companies with strong fundamentals and a clear path to profitability, rather than speculative, high-growth names. This balanced approach acknowledges the late-cycle dynamics at play while still recognizing the long-term potential of U.S. growth stocks.
